About Me

Hello! I am Mingcheng Zhang, a first-year PhD student in the Department of Applied Finance at Macquarie University. My research interests lie in Household Finance and Behavioral Finance.

I am broadly interested in how households make financial decisions, and how psychological biases and cognitive limitations shape economic behavior. I aim to combine empirical methods with insights from psychology and economics to understand real-world financial choices.

Working Papers

Fueling the Mind: How Local Gasoline Prices Shape Household Consumption
With Di Bu, and Yin Liao
Revise & Resubmit at the Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization
Abstract
We study how localized gasoline price shocks affect household consumption by linking high-frequency bank transaction data with station-level fuel prices. A 1% increase in local gasoline prices reduces monthly household spending by approximately 0.13%, even after controlling for national fuel price movements. We identify two mechanisms. First, spending declines are larger among fuel-dependent, low-liquidity households, consistent with a budget-constraint channel. Second, highly visible local price spikes reduce spending even among non-car households, consistent with a price-salience channel. We further show that localized gasoline price shocks amplify consumption inequality: low-income households with high fuel exposure experience the largest reductions, particularly in discretionary spending. Our findings show that localized energy price fluctuations shape household consumption through both financial constraints and behavioral responses to salient price signals.
Robot Adoption, Housing, and Wealth Inequality: Evidence from Chinese Household Level Data
With Penghao Cheng, Lei Lu, and Yu You
Abstract
This paper examines how robot adoption affects household asset allocation and wealth dynamics. Using Chinese household panel data, we find that automation significantly reduces household income and employment, thereby impairing asset allocation and slowing wealth accumulation. We identify the housing market as the main mechanism: declining housing wealth amplifies automation's negative impact by reducing households' willingness to invest in housing and by suppressing urban housing prices. These effects are unevenly distributed: low-income households and workers in highly automated industries suffer disproportionately larger losses, further widening wealth inequality. A life-cycle model with automation shocks to labor and housing markets supports the empirical findings and highlights their distributional implications.
